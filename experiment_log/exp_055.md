# Experiment 055: KV Cache Eviction Benchmark — Mechanistically-Informed Compression

**Cycle:** 55
**Date:** 2026-03-15
**Type:** Applied benchmark — tests whether mechanistic findings improve KV cache compression
**Models:** Qwen/Qwen3-4B-Base (n=20), meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (n=23)
**Script:** `scripts/exp_054_kv_eviction_benchmark.py` (designed cycle 54, executed cycle 55)

## Motivation

The "natural_usage" evidence bar is at 3/5 — the weakest area in the research program. Prior experiments (011-013, 015) tested noise injection at AC/TC/H2O-selected positions, but NEVER tested actual KV cache eviction (zeroing out positions from the cache). This experiment bridges 54 cycles of mechanistic insights to practical compression:

1. **K>V universal** → K-preserve strategy (keep K at all positions, only zero V at evicted)
2. **Answer head specialization** → head_selective strategy (answer heads get 2x budget)
3. **Early-position infrastructure** → early_priority strategy (keep earliest positions)
4. **Digital vs analog encoding** → compare Qwen (digital) vs Llama (analog) response to compression

## Method

1. Generate full 8-shot CoT trace with clean cache
2. Compute importance scores via K-vector L2 norms (proxy for H2O attention)
3. Find truncation point before "####" answer marker
4. Build KV cache for prompt + reasoning (single forward pass)
5. For each strategy × budget: evict positions from cache, generate answer
6. Compare answer accuracy across strategies

### Eviction Strategies

| # | Strategy | Description | Mechanistic Basis |
|---|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | random | Random position selection | Lower baseline |
| 2 | h2o | Keep positions with highest K-norm importance | H2O-like attention-based selection |
| 3 | recent | Keep first 4 (sinks) + most recent N positions | StreamingLLM approach |
| 4 | early_priority | Keep earliest positions first | Infrastructure hypothesis (Exp 028, 051) |
| 5 | k_preserve | H2O selection but keep K at evicted positions, only zero V | K>V hypothesis (Exp 023-038) |
| 6 | head_selective | Answer heads (H0/H5 Qwen, H3/H5 Llama) get 2x budget | Head specialization (Exp 045-053) |

### Cache Budgets
100% (baseline), 75%, 50%, 33% of reasoning positions kept

### Implementation Notes
- Eviction = zeroing out K+V at evicted positions (not removing from cache)
- K-preserve = zero only V at evicted positions, keep K intact for routing
- Head-selective = per-head budgets with per-head K-norm importance for position selection
- Answer generation: feed " ####" prefix, then greedy decode (stop at newline or EOS)
- Bug fix from cycle 54: updated DynamicCache API for transformers 5.3.0 (`.layers[l].keys` not `.key_cache[l]`)

### Key Finding 1: "Recent" (StreamingLLM) Is the Best Strategy on Both Models

The "recent" strategy — keeping attention sinks (first 4 positions) plus the most recent N positions — achieves perfect or near-perfect accuracy at ALL budgets on BOTH models:

| Budget | Qwen recent | Llama recent | Next best (Llama) |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| 75% | 100% | 100% | 100% (H2O, head_sel) |
| 50% | 100% | 95.7% | 91.3% (head_sel) |
| 33% | 100% | **100%** | **87.0%** (head_sel) |

At 33% budget on Llama, "recent" achieves 100% while H2O achieves only 78.3% — a +21.7pp advantage. This was NOT predicted in any pre-registered scenario.

**Mechanistic interpretation:** The "recent" strategy naturally captures both elements the model needs:
1. **Attention sinks** (first 4 positions) — routing infrastructure, consistent with the early-position infrastructure finding from Exp 028/051
2. **Recent positions** (near the answer) — the computation state immediately before the answer, where answer-relevant aggregation occurs

This is exactly the StreamingLLM architecture, and our mechanistic findings explain WHY it works: early positions provide K-routing infrastructure, while late positions carry the final computational state.

### Key Finding 2: Early_priority Is Catastrophically Bad

Keeping the earliest positions and discarding the latest is the worst strategy by far:

| Budget | Qwen early | Llama early | Qwen random | Llama random |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| 75% | 75.0% | 65.2% | 90.0% | 95.7% |
| 50% | 35.0% | 34.8% | 95.0% | 87.0% |
| 33% | 35.0% | **8.7%** | 95.0% | 69.6% |

Early_priority is WORSE than random at all budgets on both models. At 33% on Llama, it achieves only 8.7% — near-total failure.

**Mechanistic interpretation:** This is NOT contradictory with the cascading findings (Exp 051-052). Those experiments showed that CORRUPTING early positions cascades — but here we are KEEPING early positions and REMOVING late ones. Both findings are consistent:
- Early positions are infrastructure (corrupting them cascades; keeping them is necessary but not sufficient)
- Late positions carry the actual answer computation (removing them destroys the answer)
- You need BOTH: infrastructure (early) + computation (late)

The "recent" strategy succeeds precisely because it preserves both extremes.

### Key Finding 3: K-preserve Helps on Qwen but Hurts on Llama

| Budget | Qwen k_preserve | Qwen H2O | Llama k_preserve | Llama H2O |
|--------|----------------|----------|-----------------|----------|
| 75% | 100% | 100% | 95.7% | 100% |
| 50% | 100% | 100% | 82.6% | 87.0% |
| 33% | 100% | 100% | **47.8%** | **78.3%** |

On Qwen, K-preserve = H2O = 100% at all budgets. V-content is fully redundant — zeroing V at evicted positions has zero effect because K-routing alone determines computation.

On Llama, K-preserve is WORSE than H2O at 33% (47.8% vs 78.3%). Keeping K at evicted positions while zeroing V creates "phantom routing" — the model routes attention to positions that have no content, corrupting the output. On analog models, K and V are tightly coupled; K-routing without V-content is worse than full eviction (which removes both K and V, at least preventing the model from attending to empty positions).

**This is a novel and mechanistically important finding:** The K>V hierarchy established in perturbation experiments (where destroying K is worse than destroying V) does NOT translate directly to compression. In the perturbation context, V-only damage is recoverable because K-routing redirects to intact V content elsewhere. In the eviction context, K-preserve creates misleading routing — the model attends to positions that can't provide useful information.

### Key Finding 4: Head-selective Is the Best Mechanistically-Informed Strategy

On Llama, head_selective (answer heads get 2x budget, others reduced) consistently outperforms H2O:

| Budget | Llama head_sel | Llama H2O | Gap |
|--------|---------------|----------|-----|
| 75% | 100% | 100% | 0pp |
| 50% | 91.3% | 87.0% | +4.3pp |
| 33% | **87.0%** | **78.3%** | **+8.7pp** |

The advantage grows at tighter budgets (+8.7pp at 33%). Prioritizing answer heads for retention directly translates the answer-head specialization finding (Exp 045-053) into a compression advantage.

On Qwen, head_selective drops to 85% at 33% while H2O maintains 100%. The per-head budget allocation on Qwen may under-allocate to dispensable heads that still contribute to overall accuracy.

### Key Finding 5: Qwen Is Much More Compression-Robust Than Llama

| Budget | Qwen best | Qwen worst | Llama best | Llama worst |
|--------|----------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| 75% | 100% | 75% | 100% | 65.2% |
| 50% | 100% | 35% | 95.7% | 34.8% |
| 33% | 100% | 35% | 100% | 8.7% |

Excluding early_priority (worst strategy for both):
- Qwen: 85-100% at all budgets (random at 33% = 95%!)
- Llama: 47.8-100% at 33% (much wider range)

**Mechanistic interpretation:** Digital encoding (Qwen) provides position-independent codewords — each position carries self-contained information, so removing positions doesn't cascade. Analog encoding (Llama) has distributed, coupled information, so removing positions disrupts the interconnected state.

### Key Finding 6: Random Eviction Is Surprisingly Robust on Qwen

Qwen random at 33%: 95.0% — removing 67% of reasoning positions randomly barely affects accuracy.

This is consistent with the digital encoding model: if each position carries a self-contained codeword, losing any given position is recoverable from the remaining positions' codewords. The information is redundantly encoded.

## Self-Review

### Strengths
1. **First actual eviction test** in the program — all prior compression experiments used noise injection
2. **6 strategies × 3 budgets × 2 models** = 36 non-trivial conditions
3. **Clear practical implications** — "recent" and "head_selective" are actionable compression strategies
4. **K-preserve reversal** is a novel finding that refines the K>V interpretation
5. **Cross-model comparison** reveals encoding-dependent compression behavior

### Limitations
1. **n=20 (Qwen) and n=23 (Llama)** — adequate for large effects but CIs are wide for close comparisons
2. **H2O proxy uses K-norms, not actual attention weights** — true H2O uses cumulative attention, which requires output_attentions (OOM on 36-layer model). K-norm is a reasonable proxy but may not exactly replicate H2O performance.
3. **Eviction = zeroing, not true removal** — zeroed positions still participate in softmax normalization, diluting attention. True eviction (removing from cache) would concentrate attention on remaining positions. This may understate the effectiveness of some strategies.
4. **"recent" strategy has a structural advantage** — it always keeps the last positions, which are closest to the answer marker. In the GSM8K format, these positions contain the final calculation step. The strategy may be less effective on tasks where the answer depends on early reasoning steps.

### Confounds
1. **K-norm proxy vs true H2O:** If K-norms systematically differ from cumulative attention at key positions, the H2O strategy may be under- or over-performing relative to true H2O. However, K-norms and attention should be strongly correlated (high K-norm → larger QK dot product → higher attention).
2. **Zeroing vs masking:** Zeroed positions contribute to the softmax denominator, effectively adding uniform noise. True eviction would remove them from attention entirely. The "k_preserve" strategy is particularly affected — keeping K at zeroed-V positions creates misleading routing.
3. **GSM8K format bias:** "Recent" benefits from GSM8K's sequential computation where the last step produces the answer. On tasks requiring information from across the trace (e.g., multi-hop QA), "recent" might perform worse.

### Alternative Explanations
1. "Recent works because the answer token attends most to nearby positions (recency bias)." This is partially true — attention naturally has a recency gradient. But the specific combination of sinks + recent is what makes StreamingLLM effective, and our findings explain the mechanistic basis.
2. "K-preserve fails on Llama because zeroing V adds noise, not because of routing mismatch." This is possible — zeroing V at a position the model attends to effectively corrupts the attention output. The K-routing was never "wrong" — it's the V-content absence that causes failure. This actually REINFORCES the V-content importance on analog models.

### Evidential Strength

- "Recent" (StreamingLLM) is the best compression strategy: **Strong** (100% at 33% on Llama; 100% across all on Qwen)
- Early-priority is catastrophically bad: **Strong** (8.7% at 33% Llama; worse than random everywhere)
- K-preserve does NOT translate to compression advantage: **Moderate** (clear on Llama, null effect on Qwen)
- Head-selective outperforms H2O: **Moderate** (+8.7pp at 33% Llama, but n=23 with wide CIs)
- Digital encoding is more compression-robust: **Moderate** (Qwen random@33%=95% vs Llama=70%, consistent with prior findings)
