# Experiment 002: Double Dissociation via KV Position Pruning (retry)

**Cycle:** 2
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Type:** Experiment
**Model:** Qwen/Qwen3-4B-Base
**Script:** `scripts/exp_002_double_dissociation.py`

## Motivation

This is a retry of Experiment 001, which failed due to two bugs:
1. Used `Qwen/Qwen3-4B` (instruct model) instead of `Qwen/Qwen3-4B-Base` — the instruct model expects chat templates, not raw few-shot prompts, so it failed to solve any GSM8K problems
2. No stop criteria — the model continued generating new Q&A pairs instead of stopping after `####`

The double dissociation under normal inference is the most critical untested prediction. Prior experiments (Exp 4-5) showed PGD adversarial perturbations concentrate at answer-coupled positions (rho=0.78) and avoid text-coupled positions. But a skeptical reviewer could argue the PGD optimizer *creates* the separation rather than discovering it.

## Method

1. Generate 8-shot CoT traces for 30 GSM8K problems (greedy decoding)
2. Teacher-force each trace to extract attention weights (last 4 layers)
3. Compute per-position answer-coupling (attention from final token) and text-coupling (average attention from later reasoning tokens)
4. Prune top 25% of positions for each category by zeroing KV cache entries
5. Regenerate answer from pruned cache; measure accuracy and text prediction loss

### Conditions
- **Control (no pruning):** Baseline — teacher-forced trace, generate answer
- **Prune answer-coupled:** Zero KV at top-25% answer-coupling positions
- **Prune text-coupled:** Zero KV at top-25% text-coupling positions
- **Prune random:** Zero KV at random 25% of positions (matched count)

### Fixes from exp_001
- Correct model: `Qwen/Qwen3-4B-Base` (base model, proper in-context learning)
- Stop on `\nQ:` patterns to prevent continuation past the answer
- Stop on `####` + number completion
- Sequence length cap (2048) for attention extraction to prevent OOM
- Answer normalization for comparison (handles decimals, commas)

## Pre-Registered Predictions

### If hypothesis is TRUE (functionally separable channels):
- **Prune answer-coupled:** Accuracy drops significantly (≤60%), text loss change near zero (≤+0.05 nats)
- **Prune text-coupled:** Accuracy preserved (≥80%), text loss increases substantially (≥+0.1 nats)
- **Prune random:** Intermediate effects on both metrics
- **Double dissociation pattern:** AC accuracy < TC accuracy AND TC text-loss-change > AC text-loss-change

### If hypothesis is FALSE (no functional separation):
- All pruning conditions affect accuracy and text loss similarly
- No dissociation: pruning any positions degrades both channels equally
- The difference between conditions is within noise (effect sizes < 0.1)

### This experiment is informative because:
It tests functional separability under normal inference, without adversarial optimization. If the dissociation appears here, it cannot be an artifact of the PGD procedure — it reflects genuine architectural separation of information channels.

## Execution Notes

- **Actual config:** NUM_PROBLEMS=90, PRUNE_FRACTION=0.50 (50%, not 25% as initially designed)
- Baseline accuracy: 82/90 (91.1%)
- Valid problems (baseline correct, no OOM): 82
- Elapsed: 489s (~8 min)

## Results

**NEGATIVE RESULT — No double dissociation found.**

All pruning conditions achieved 100% answer accuracy (82/82 valid problems):

| Condition | Accuracy | Text Loss Change (nats) |
|-----------|----------|------------------------|
| Control (no pruning) | 100% (82/82) | +0.000 |
| Prune answer-coupled (top 50%) | 100% (82/82) | +0.506 ± 0.344 |
| Prune text-coupled (top 50%) | 100% (82/82) | +0.527 ± 0.370 |
| Prune random (50%) | 100% (82/82) | +0.225 ± 0.300 |

### Double dissociation test:
- Accuracy dissociation (AC drops more than TC): **NO** (both 100%)
- Text loss dissociation (TC increases more than AC): **YES** (+0.527 vs +0.506) — but marginal
- Full double dissociation: **NO**

### Prediction outcome: **DISCONFIRMED (hypothesis prediction)**

The hypothesis predicted pruning answer-coupled positions would drop accuracy to ≤60%. Instead, 100% accuracy was maintained. Neither the FALSE prediction pattern (equal degradation) was observed — there was NO degradation at all.

## Interpretation

Zeroing 50% of KV positions (by any classification) does not affect answer accuracy. This suggests:

1. **The model routes around zeroed positions** — attention is soft, and remaining positions carry sufficient information. Zeroed KV entries are effectively ignored by the attention mechanism, and the model uses the other 50% plus the 20 re-processed lookback tokens to recover.

2. **Answer information is highly distributed** — not concentrated at specific positions. The PGD correlation (rho=0.78) may reflect that the optimizer preferentially uses high-attention positions because they have more gradient signal, not because the information is stored there.

3. **The methodology may be too weak** — zeroing is not the same as removing. Zero-valued KV entries still participate in attention computation (they contribute zero to the weighted sum but still enter the softmax denominator). A stronger test would be position removal, noise injection, or much higher pruning fractions.

The fact that random pruning causes less text loss increase (+0.225) than targeted pruning (+0.506/+0.527) is noteworthy: it suggests that high-coupling positions (both AC and TC) are disproportionately important for text prediction, while the specific AC/TC distinction doesn't differentiate their effects.

## Self-Review

- **Evidential strength: Weak negative** — the null result could be methodological (zeroing too weak) rather than substantive
- **Confound:** The lookback mechanism re-processes 20 tokens through the pruned cache, potentially allowing recovery
- **Alternative explanation:** Zeroing allows routing around; position REMOVAL or noise injection might produce different results
- **Next step:** Pruning fraction sweep (50% → 95%) to find the cliff, or noise injection at classified positions
- **Statistical concerns:** 82 valid problems is adequate, but all-or-nothing results (100%) provide no variance for statistical testing
