# Experiment 011: H2O Heavy-Hitter vs Answer-Coupled Position Overlap

**Cycle:** 11
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Type:** Correlation / natural usage analysis
**Models:** Qwen3-4B-Base (primary), Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (replication)
**Script:** `scripts/exp_011_h2o_overlap.py`

## Motivation

All prior evidence for the hidden channel comes from artificial/adversarial perturbations (PGD, noise injection, zeroing). The "natural_usage" progress bar sits at 2/5 with only literature connections. Open question #20 from the ledger asks: "Do H2O heavy-hitter positions correlate with our answer-coupled positions?"

H2O (Heavy-Hitter Oracle) is a practical KV cache compression method that retains positions with the highest cumulative attention from all subsequent tokens. If H2O heavy-hitters overlap with our answer-coupled (AC) positions, this proves that working compression methods already implicitly preserve the hidden channel. This would:
1. Connect our basic science to a practical application
2. Explain WHY H2O-style compression works (it preserves the answer channel)
3. Validate our AC/TC classification framework against an independent importance metric

## Method

For each model (Qwen3-4B-Base, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct):
1. Generate 8-shot CoT traces for 25 GSM8K problems, filter to baseline-correct
2. Teacher-force traces with `output_attentions=True` (full forward pass)
3. For each position in the reasoning trace, compute:
   a. **H2O score**: Cumulative attention received from ALL subsequent tokens across ALL layers and heads
   b. **AC score**: Attention from the last (answer) token to each position (last 4 layers, as in exp_004/005)
   c. **TC score**: Average attention from later reasoning tokens to each position (last 4 layers)
   d. **Selectivity**: rank(AC) - rank(TC) per position
4. Compute correlations:
   - H2O vs AC (Spearman, Pearson) — Do heavy-hitters = answer-coupled positions?
   - H2O vs TC — Do heavy-hitters = text-coupled positions?
   - H2O vs selectivity — Do heavy-hitters = answer-SPECIFIC positions?
5. Simulate compression at retention rates 10%, 20%, ..., 90%:
   - H2O retention: keep top-k by H2O score
   - AC retention: keep top-k by AC score
   - Compute Jaccard overlap between retained sets
   - Compute fraction of AC-selective positions kept under H2O retention
6. Phase 2 (if time): Actual ablation — noise-replace evicted positions under H2O vs AC vs random retention and measure accuracy

## Pre-Registered Predictions

### If H2O heavy-hitters ≈ AC positions (practical validation):
- Spearman correlation H2O vs AC: >= 0.6
- Jaccard overlap at 50% retention: >= 0.5
- H2O compression preferentially retains AC positions over TC positions
- Implication: existing compression methods implicitly preserve the hidden channel

### If H2O heavy-hitters ≈ TC positions (compression may harm hidden channel):
- Spearman correlation H2O vs TC: >= 0.6 AND H2O vs AC: <= 0.3
- H2O compression preferentially retains TC positions over AC positions
- Implication: existing compression methods may destroy the answer channel

### If H2O heavy-hitters are a mix (partial preservation):
- Both correlations moderate (0.3-0.6)
- H2O retains both AC and TC positions at similar rates
- Implication: compression methods partially preserve the hidden channel

### Based on our existing finding that AC positions are hubs:
- **Most likely prediction**: H2O vs AC correlation will be HIGH (>= 0.6)
- Reason: AC positions are hubs that receive attention from both answer and text tokens. Since H2O measures cumulative attention from ALL tokens, hubs should dominate the H2O ranking.
- The more interesting question is whether H2O vs SELECTIVITY is positive or near-zero. If positive, H2O preferentially keeps answer-SPECIFIC positions. If zero, H2O just keeps hubs.

### Specific quantitative predictions:
- H2O vs AC Spearman: 0.6-0.8 (high, because hubs)
- H2O vs TC Spearman: 0.4-0.7 (moderate, because hubs contribute to TC too)
- H2O vs selectivity Spearman: 0.1-0.3 (weak, because H2O doesn't distinguish AC-specific from hubs)
- Jaccard overlap (H2O top-50% vs AC top-50%): 0.4-0.7
- These patterns should replicate across both models

### This experiment is informative because:
1. Tests whether practical KV compression implicitly preserves the hidden channel
2. Provides "natural usage" evidence — H2O was designed for efficiency, not for our hypothesis
3. Disambiguates "AC positions are hubs" from "AC positions are answer-specific"
4. If H2O vs selectivity is high, it suggests something deeper about attention-based importance
5. Cross-model comparison tests whether the relationship holds across architectures

At 50% retention, H2O-selected positions contain:
- **Qwen:** 43.6% AC-selective, 54.6% TC-selective

Both models: H2O preferentially retains TC-selective positions (57-55%) over AC-selective (40-44%). Baseline expectation: 50/50.

## Interpretation

### Why H2O ≠ AC despite AC positions being "hubs"

The "AC positions are hubs" finding from exp_004/005 is about attention from the last few reasoning tokens AND the answer token. But H2O measures cumulative attention from ALL subsequent tokens across ALL layers. These capture fundamentally different things:

1. **H2O is dominated by position**: In causal attention, earlier positions are attended to by more subsequent tokens (they have more tokens "downstream"). So H2O score is strongly correlated with position — early tokens get high H2O scores simply because many tokens follow them.

2. **AC score captures answer-specific attention**: The answer token (last position) attends selectively to positions that computed relevant intermediate results. These tend to be later positions (closer to the answer computation).

3. **The clash**: H2O favors EARLY positions (high cumulative attention from many subsequent tokens). AC favors LATE positions (close to where the answer is computed). These are opposite ends of the sequence.

### Practical implication

**H2O-style compression may actively harm the hidden channel.** By retaining positions with the highest cumulative attention (early, text-scaffolding positions) and evicting positions with the lowest cumulative attention (late, answer-relevant positions), H2O may preserve text coherence at the expense of answer accuracy.

This suggests a new compression strategy: **AC-aware compression** that explicitly retains answer-coupled positions (identified by attention from the final token) rather than generic heavy-hitters. This could improve accuracy under aggressive compression.

### Important caveat: positional confound

The H2O-AC dissociation may be partly an artifact of the positional structure of causal attention. A more rigorous test would control for position (e.g., compare H2O and AC rankings within position bins). However, even if positionally confounded, the practical conclusion stands: H2O's importance ranking diverges from the answer-relevant positions.

## Self-Review

### Confounds
1. **Qwen n=4**: Very low; Qwen results are not statistically meaningful. Focus on Llama.
2. **Positional confound**: H2O is dominated by position (early = high). AC may also be position-dependent (late = high due to recency). The anti-correlation could be partly artifactual.
3. **H2O implementation**: Real H2O implementations work per-layer with fixed budgets, not aggregated across all layers. Our aggregated H2O score may not exactly match production H2O behavior.
4. **AC definition**: Using all heads in last 4 layers. The PGD perturbation correlation (rho=0.78) used answer-selective heads, which may give a different signal.
5. **Base vs Instruct**: Qwen is base, Llama is Instruct. Instruction tuning may affect attention patterns.

### Alternative explanations
1. **H2O and AC measure orthogonal importance dimensions**: "Popularity" (H2O) vs "relevance to answer" (AC). Both are valid measures but capture different aspects of token importance.
2. **Causal attention creates a structural divergence**: By construction, early tokens get high H2O scores and late tokens get low ones. If answer computation concentrates in late positions, any cumulative-attention metric will miss it.
3. **H2O works for text quality, not answer accuracy**: H2O was designed for generation quality, which may correlate more with text-coupling than answer-coupling. Compression methods may primarily preserve text fluency while implicitly sacrificing some answer accuracy.

### Statistical concerns
- Qwen n=4: unreliable, wide CIs. Treat as directionally suggestive only.
- Llama n=17: adequate. H2O vs AC rho=0.11 is significantly different from zero (p=8.7e-5) but far from the predicted 0.6-0.8.
- Pooled analysis (1258 positions for Llama) has good statistical power.

### Evidential strength: moderate (surprising negative result)
- Clearly disconfirms the prediction that H2O ≈ AC
- Replicated directionally on both models (despite Qwen n=4)
- Positional confound is a concern but doesn't change practical conclusion
- The composition analysis (57% TC-selective in H2O retention) provides clear evidence
- The finding is novel and practically relevant for KV cache compression
